THE AGORA

A discourse engine for contested questions

The Agora is a tool for thinking about things that resist easy answers. You bring a question, a claim, a policy, a belief — anything contested — and the Agora generates a structured discourse where competing perspectives genuinely contend with each other.

This is not "both sides" journalism. Each perspective argues its strongest case, exposes weaknesses in opposing positions, and identifies where fundamental principles collide. The goal is not resolution. The goal is clarity about what the real disagreements are.

The discourse is shaped by eleven philosophical principles — a framework for examining how we think, not what to think.

The Eleven Principles

The Agora's discourse is shaped by a framework of eleven principles. These are not rules for what to believe — they are lenses for examining how we think. Each discourse identifies which principles are invoked, which are violated, and which are placed under pressure by the topic at hand.

About the Agora

The Agora is a project of stillthinking.org, an independent space for philosophical inquiry and analytical frameworks.

The name draws from the ancient Greek ἀγορά — the public square where citizens gathered to debate, trade, and contest ideas. The original agora was not a place of consensus. It was a place where competing visions of the good life were forced into contact with each other.

This tool exists because the quality of public discourse is degrading. Social media algorithms reward certainty, outrage, and tribal affiliation. Nuance is punished. Complexity is flattened. The Agora is an attempt to move in the opposite direction — to create a space where ideas are stress-tested rather than applauded, where the goal is to identify genuine tensions rather than to win arguments.

The eleven principles that shape each discourse were developed over many years through a process of philosophical reflection, testing, and revision. They are not presented as universal truths but as tools — ways of interrogating one's own thinking and the thinking of others.

The discourse engine is powered by AI. This is deliberate: AI can simulate perspectives without ego, tribal loyalty, or fear of social consequence. It can argue positions it doesn't hold with full force. It can identify principle conflicts that human debaters might avoid because they're uncomfortable. The AI is a tool in service of the framework, not a replacement for human judgement.

The Agora does not tell you what to think. It shows you what you'd need to accept — and what you'd need to give up — to hold any given position consistently.

Built by a financial planner and philosophical framework developer in Perth, Western Australia. Because the best thinking often comes from people whose day job is something else entirely.
